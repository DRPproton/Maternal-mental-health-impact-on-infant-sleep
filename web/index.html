Maternal Mental Health & Infant Sleep

A Data-Driven Exploration of Postpartum Outcomes

1. The Unseen Toll: An Introduction

This report investigates the complex relationship between infant sleep patterns, infant temperament, and maternal mental health outcomes—specifically Postpartum Depression (PPD), Clinical Anxiety, and Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) stemming from childbirth. This study uses a cohort of 410 mothers of infants aged 3 to 12 months. It proceeds through exploratory data analysis, statistical hypothesis testing, unsupervised clustering, and supervised machine learning to predict maternal distress.

2. Translating Data into Diagnostics

The sociodemographic foundation of the cohort is heavily characterized by mothers in their early-to-mid 30s who are highly educated (possessing university or associate/tech degrees) and predominantly in stable relationships. This demographic uniformity is visually confirmed in the Marital Status Distribution and demographic histograms.

Clinical mental health outcomes were evaluated using established diagnostic scoring rubrics:

  • Postpartum Depression (EPDS): An extreme class imbalance was detected, with 95.6% of the cohort scoring 11 or higher, indicating a high clinical risk for PPD.
  • Clinical Anxiety (HADS-A): The cohort exhibited a well-distributed anxiety profile: 52.4% scored in the "Normal" range, 21.7% "Borderline," and 25.9% "Abnormal".
  • Birth Trauma (CBTS): The average PTSD score was 13.1, but the top quartile scored 19 or above, revealing a severe tail of unresolved birth trauma among a subset of mothers.
Critical Finding: 95.6% of mothers in this dataset breached the clinical threshold for Postpartum Depression (EPDS score >= 11).

3. The Baseline: Demographics & Statistics

Bivariate statistical analyses were conducted to determine if demographic factors intrinsically influence mental health deterioration.

  • A T-Test examining maternal anxiety variance based on the biological sex of the infant yielded no statistical significance (p = 0.432), a finding illustrated in the Maternal Anxiety by Infant Sex boxplot.
  • Similarly, an ANOVA testing the impact of marital status on PPD risk found no significant variance (p = 0.148), visualized in the Maternal PPD by Marital Status distribution.

Conversely, Pearson correlations mathematically confirmed the physical impact of infant sleep on the mother. There is an extremely statistically significant linear relationship (p = 2.86e-06) between shorter total infant night sleep duration and higher maternal PPD scores. However, the frequency of night awakenings did not significantly correlate with childbirth PTSD (p = 0.206), suggesting that trauma is driven by independent birth complications rather than ongoing sleep fragmentation.

Infant sleep characteristics were separated into two distinct physiological stressors: total duration and sleep fragmentation. While most infants achieved 10 to 11 hours of sleep, a distinct left tail indicated a subset struggling to reach 8 hours. Additionally, night awakenings demonstrated a right-skewed distribution; while 1 to 2 awakenings were standard, a clinical tail of infants woke 4 to 6+ times a night.

The Infant Behavior Questionnaire (IBQ) revealed three naturally emerging psychological clusters regarding infant temperament, confirmed via the Correlation Matrix of IBQ Items:

  1. Social Fear / Stranger Anxiety: High correlation (r = 0.76 - 0.81) for infants clinging to parents around unfamiliar adults.
  2. Sleep-Onset Anger: Strong correlation (r = 0.65), highlighting infants who actively fight sleep with crying and fussing.
  3. Frustration / Attention Seeking: Moderate-to-strong correlation (r = 0.50) mapping to general frustration tolerance.

4. Algorithmically Discovered Profiles

An unsupervised K-Means algorithm successfully partitioned the mothers into three distinct behavioral profiles, visualized in the Discovered Maternal Profiles PPD vs Infant Sleep scatter plot. This clustering proved that sleep deprivation is not the sole trigger for maternal distress:

  • Profile 0 (The High-Sleep / High-Depression Paradox): Infants achieved excellent sleep (10.85 hours, 0.68 awakenings), yet mothers exhibited the highest rates of severe PPD (EPDS = 18.79), proving PPD can manifest aggressively without sleep deprivation.
  • Profile 1 (The Fragmented Sleep Cluster): Aligns with the physical exhaustion hypothesis. Infants had the lowest sleep duration (9.11 hours) and the highest awakenings (3.00), directly translating to severe maternal PPD.
  • Profile 2 (The Severe Trauma & Clinical Anxiety Cluster): Infant sleep was moderate, but maternal HADS-A (12.61) and CBTS (26.46) scores were explosively high, marking a distinct psychiatric emergency driven by birth trauma.

5. Predicting the Crisis

The final phase successfully deployed supervised machine learning to predict Maternal Clinical Anxiety Risk (Normal, Borderline, Abnormal). To prevent overfitting, the feature space was pruned to the top 15 most mathematically powerful predictors using Gini Importance, heavily favoring survey variables related to panic, worry, and fear. These key variables are ranked in the Top 15 Most Predictive Features for Maternal Anxiety chart.

A highly visual Decision Tree was optimized using a 5-Fold GridSearchCV (max_depth: 6, criterion: 'gini'), yielding an accuracy of 78.0%. The branching logic of this model is mapped in the AI Decision Logic: Diagnosing Maternal Anxiety diagram.

When tested against an unseen 20% validation set, a Random Forest Ensemble emerged as the champion algorithm with an 87.8% accuracy. The model demonstrated 95% precision for diagnosing "Abnormal" anxiety and 95% recall for identifying "Normal" anxiety, though it struggled moderately (75% precision) with the vague threshold of "Borderline" cases.

Conclusion

The structured data framework validates the core mathematical thesis: the temperament and sleep quality of an infant serve as quantifiable, statistically powerful predictors for clinical postnatal distress. By combining psychometric clinical scoring with machine learning, this study successfully engineered a diagnostic pipeline capable of identifying maternal anxiety risk with near 88% accuracy.
